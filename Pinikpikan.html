Pinikpikan

The traditional "killing me softly" chicken dish.

Cultural Background

Pinikpikan is a dish from the mountains of the Cordillera region in the Philippines. It is controversial to some due to its preparation method, but it holds deep cultural significance. The chicken is beaten with a stick to coagulate the blood under the skin, which gives the meat a darker color and a richer, smokier flavor when cooked.

Ingredients

  • 1 whole native chicken
  • Etag (salted/smoked pork)
  • Sayote (chayote) or papaya
  • Ginger
  • Pechay (bok choy) or watercress
  • Salt and pepper to taste

Cooking Process

  1. Prepare the chicken by beating it lightly with a stick to bruise the meat (traditionally done while alive, but modern versions may differ).
  2. Burn the feathers off over an open fire to add a smoky aroma.
  3. Butcher the chicken and cut into serving pieces.
  4. Boil water in a pot and add the chicken and Etag. Simmer until tender.
  5. Add ginger and vegetables (sayote/papaya first, then leafy greens).
  6. Season with salt and pepper. Serve hot with rice.

Personal Insights

Eating Pinikpikan is not just about the taste; it's about the experience of sharing a meal that has been part of rituals and gatherings for centuries. The smoky flavor of the burnt feathers and the saltiness of the Etag create a unique broth that warms the soul in the cold mountain weather.
